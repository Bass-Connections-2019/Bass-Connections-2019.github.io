Project Background

Access to electricity is one of the most important requisites to economic and societal development. However, over 1 billion people lack access to electricity. To enable electrification and grid expansion in energy-poor regions, it is crucial to know where the existing infrastructure is. Current approaches to identifying and mapping energy infrastructure tend to be expensive and time intensive.

Previous Progress

Previous Bass Connections teams have worked on developing deep learning models that identify energy infrastructure, with an end goal of generating maps of power grid networks that can aid policy makers in implementing effective electrification strategies.

However, their models have shown bad adaptation with respect to different geographic domains. Challenges associated with satellite imagery with different geographies: (from left) Vienna, Tyrol, Austin, Chicago. Figures are from the INRIA dataset.

This year, we want our model to function across all geographies, so we can scale around the globe. We are accomplishing this through a technique called domain adaptation. To do this, we need techniques to visualize how our model is working along the way, and use that information to help us improve our model training process. We will also need to create and add synthetic imagery to compensate for the lack of domain diversity in our training data.

Our Goal

Image Segmentation

• Model Iterative Process.   • Cross-Domain Accuracy.   • Features Visualization through T-SNE.

Image Segmentation is a deep learning model which can segment images and identify target objects at scale by assigning each pixel to a probability. Satellite imagery can thus be simplified into analyzable structural maps and offer insights on the model's generalizability across different geographic domains.

We chose Models for Remote Sensing (MRS), an encoder-decoder model to perform the segmentation. To scope into the problem of cross-geographical adaptation, we trained and tested the MRS2 model using a geography-by-geography basis on the INRIA dataset1, which contains urban satellite imagery for different cities across the world.

Model Iterative Process

The input satellite image from the Inria dataset is fed through the CNN to give an output segmented image. We are extracting feature visualizations from the second to last layer of the model and using those visualizations to evaluate the segmentation capability of our model, adding synthetic imagery as needed.

Cross-Domain Accuracy

As shown in the table, the most accurate model for a given city is the model that is trained on that same city, and models trained on a single city don’t necessarily generalize well to the other cities. Furthermore, we found that training a model on all of the cities results in higher test accuracies across the board. To address this problem of transferring learning across geographic domains we want to: Visualize the model’s representation of the current training domain(Goal 2). Provide ways to diversify this training domain (Goal 3).

Table: Evaluation of models trained on each individual city from the Inria dataset, as well as a model trained on the entire dataset. The models are then evaluated on each city (columns), and results are measured in IoU (Intersection over Union). Orange cells represent the best model for each city, while green cells represent best performance out of all models.

Features Visualization through T-SNE

Feature visualization is the process of visualizing how a decision system ‘interprets’ images. A robust recognition model would distinguish images based on the existence of target infrastructure, and be agnostic to differences in geographical differences. The goal is to take the current feature representation, which clusters by location, and instead be able to cluster by presence or absence of infrastructure.

In order to visualize the feature representation of our current model, we need to reduce high-dimensional feature vectors to lower dimensions. We achieve this using a reputed technique, t-SNE3 (t-distributed Stochastic Neighbor Embedding), to reduce the high-dimensional, intermediate feature map (neural layer output) of our dataset to a low-dimensional (2-D) space while preserving the local structure of the features. Using t-SNE, we wish to see how our model represents our data domain, and transform this to become geography-agnostic. For the plots below, each dot is an image patch from the city represented by a specific color.

The model visibly separates (left figure) Chicago and Tyrol. But for the other 3 cities, the model does not give explicitly separated clusters, and thus does not differentiate well amongst the images from these different geographies. The middle figure consists of Kitsap and Vienna, and the right figure displays all 5 cities. We aim to investigate this separation by geography, and compare it to t-SNE feature visualization for data diversified using synthetic imagery.
